WORKFLOWS

Structure Overview:

  • The authors created PyRate to provide a robust probabilistic method to infer diversification dynamics from fossil occurrences, which can be preferable to using phylogenetic data alone, especially when extinction rates are high.
  • Structure:
    • PyRate requires some a priori decision inputs from the user, but default values and settings are set to adapt to most datasets.
    • PyRate runs as a stand-alone command-line program
  • Input: PyRate requires a table (tab-delimited text file) containing species names, their extant or extinct status, and the minimum and maximum ages of their fossil occurrences. An optional trait value can be included for each species for trait-dependent diversification analyses.

Fossil occurrence data (from online) → PyRate →

  1. Speciation (birth/origination) rates, variable through time
  2. Extinction (death) rates, variable through time
    1. Speciation and extinction times: Birth-Death model. PyRate estimates the true times of speciation and extinction for each lineage based on the preservation process, as the first and last fossil appearances may not represent the actual origination and extinction times.
  3. Preservation rates, variable through time (accounts for preservation biases) → Preservation model. PyRate models the preservation process, which describes how likely a lineage is to be preserved as a fossil record over time. The preservation rate is the expected number of fossil occurrences per lineage per million years. PyRate can account for heterogeneity in preservation rates across lineages using the gamma model.

Modelling:

  • Model testing: PyRate uses Bayes factors to compare the fit of alternative birth-death models (e.g., different numbers of rate shifts, with or without trait correlation) by estimating their marginal likelihoods using thermodynamic integration.
  • MCMC algorithms: PyRate employs Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods to jointly estimate the model parameters, including the preservation rates, speciation and extinction times, and birth-death rates. The BDMCMC algorithm can estimate the number and temporal placement of rate shifts and marginal rates through time.
  • Output: PyRate generates log files containing the posterior samples of model parameters, marginal likelihoods, and marginal rates through time.
    • These files can be analyzed using programs like Tracer to assess convergence, determine burn-in, and summarize posterior estimates.

Visualization:

  • Outputs R scripts to create plots of the estimated speciation, extinction, and net diversification rates through time, with 95% credibility intervals.
  • Additional plots can be generated to visualize the correlation between rates and continuous traits.

General Workflow

Here's a step-wise order of how PyRate works:

  1. **Input Data**: PyRate reads the input fossil occurrence data, which includes the taxa names and the temporal ranges of fossil species. Raw data came w/ temporal ranges of each fossil, we parse data by species.
  2. **Preservation Model Specification**: The user specifies the type of preservation model (NHPP, HPP, TPP, or Gamma) and any additional parameters required for the chosen model. The model estimates the probability of a species being preserved in the fossil record, i.e., preservation rates.
    1. Poisson process
  3. **Likelihood Calculation**: PyRate calculates the likelihood of the observed fossil occurrences under the specified preservation model. The likelihood function depends on the model type and the parameters of the model.
  4. ** Birth-Death Models**: PyRate choses BD, BDS, or BDMCMC model to estimate speciation rates and extinction rates over time. These draw out the diversification dynamics of the lineages over time. Imagine the phylogeny
    1. Each species is a poisson processes where the shift from birth→ death are transitions between ‘diversification states’ modelled as Continuous-time Markov Processes
  5. **Parameter Joint Estimation**: PyRate uses MCMC methods to jointly the parameters of both the Preservation models and the Birth-Death models. By combining both model types, PyRate is able to integrate both the diversification process and the preservation process
    1. The MCMC algorithm constructs a Markov chain that explores the parameter space and converges to the posterior distribution of the model parameters.
    2. During each iteration of the MCMC algorithm, the parameters of the preservation models and birth-death models are updated based on the likelihood of the data and the prior distributions of the parameters.
    3. The MCMC samples generated from the posterior distribution are then used to make inferences about the speciation, extinction, and preservation rates over time.
    4. It maximizes the likelihood of the observed data by sampling from the posterior distribution of the model parameters, considering both the fossil occurrence data and the prior distributions of the parameters.
  6. **Model Comparison: Output**: PyRate outputs the optimized model parameters, the maximum likelihood estimates of S, E and Preservation rates over time, and other relevant statistics.
    1. The output can also include log files and visualizations of the preservation rate over time and the estimated diversity trajectories.
    2. Ex: the RTT plot showing S, E and net diversification (S-E) rates

The specific code implementation for each step depends on the chosen preservation model and the optimization method. Model Estimations:

  • Calculate Likelihood P(B|A): Now you need the fossil data + the priors. Compute the likelihood of the observed fossil data given the prior distributions P(A) that we specified.
    • The prior distributions are our model parameters, which are sampled from those priors
    • Helps account for incomplete fossil preservation, helps decide which preservation model best fits the data
    • Preservation model: Poisson process, where fossil occurrences are independent and occur at a constant rate q over time
      • Preservation model likelihood: probability of observing a specific number of fossil occurrences (k) for a given species over a certain time interval (T), given the preservation rate (q), and the s and e event times
      • Likelihood calculated using Poisson probability mass function
      • Function: lik_fossil = -q * (ts - te) + k * log(q) - log_factorial_occs
      • P(B|A) = P(fossil data | q, ts, te) → how likely it is to observe fossils given particular q, ts, and te values
        • B (data): Observed fossil occurrences
        • A (parameters): Their values come from the current state of the MCMC chain, which DOES NOT sample them from user-set priors, but just proposes new values using proposal mechanisms (multiplier proposals)
          • Preservation rate/parameter (q)

P(q) is often a Gamma distribution

          • Estimated true speciation times (ts), estimated true extinction times (te)

Estimated by the BD model: P(ts) and P(te) are distributions informed by the BD model’s estimated speciation rate (λ), extinction rate (μ)

ts and te are initialized based on the observed fossil dataset:

FAD in the fossil record is the minimum age for ts

LAD in the fossil record is the maximum age for te

Then, they are informed by both the Preservation model and the BD Model, where PyRate treats them as additional parameters to be estimated. This allows PyRate to account for preservation biases that would make true s and e times uncertain/diff from FAD and LAD

    • Birth-Death Model (BD: each variable is part of a Poisson Process, but are themselves modelled as Continuous-time Markov process, where the system transitions between states (current number of species/lineages, then changes in their number due to transitions from birth → death and death → birth of lineages) continuously over time, with the rates of transition (s/birth and e/death), determining the probability of moving from one state to another
      • Birth-Death model likelihood: for a single species, the probability of observing the specific pattern of fossil occurrences (times of observed species origination S and death E events) in the data given the prior s and e rates overall, over time T
      • Likelihood calculated using probability density function of the continuous-time Markov process
      • Function: lik_BB
      • P(B|A) = P(ts, te | λ, μ) → the plausible distributions of ts and te
        • B (data): estimated true speciation times (ts), estimated true extinction times (te)
        • A (parameters): Their values come from the current state of the MCMC chain, which DOES NOT sample them from user-set priors, but just proposes new values using proposal mechanisms (multiplier proposals)
          • Estimated speciation rate (λ), extinction rate (μ)
      • RJMCMC: MCMC that allows the number and timing of rate shifts in the BD model to be sampled as well. So the # of parameters can vary during sampling, automatically adjusting model complexity based on the data
    • Results: probability of observing exactly these # of fossils in the fossil data at these specific times given our preservation rate q (the model’s parameters)
      • P(λ, μ, q, ts, te | fossil data) ∝ P(fossil data | q, ts, te) * P(ts, te | λ, μ) * P(λ) * P(μ) * P(q)
        • P(λ) * P(μ) * P(q) → user specified priors
      • Dataset with multiple species: run this likelihood calculation across all species, then multiply every species’ probability to get total likelihood
      • ^ summed in log space to avoid numerical underflow. So you get a log likelihood, which is more computationally stable for optimization and Bayesian inference
  • P(B): Marginal likelihood: The probability of observing that data under a specific birth-death model with those specific parameters, marginalized (averaged) over all many MCMC samples of the parameter space of that birth-death model. Or: probability of the data given the chosen model after we have integrated out all the parameters
    • * See Bayes Variables section
    • A measure of how well ONE particular BIRTH-DEATH model fits the observed data. The parameter set with the highest marginal likelihood = the best fit for the data Birth-Death Model Comparison using P(B): After getting P(B) for one model, you could do so for other models, then compare ratios of P(B) ratios of one model to another in a term called a Bayes Factor.
      • Bayes factors are better than likelihood ratio tests for model comparison because they inherently help to avoid overparameterization. The P(B) of a very complex model (which has many more parameters than the other model it is being compared with in its Bayes Factor), will be smaller than the P(B) of a more complex model
      • This is because the calculation of P(B) is a more complex equation than usual Bayes, P(B | M) = ∫ P(B | θ, M) P(θ | M) dθ. That involves the prior distribution and the likelihood, both of which are more spread out in complex models, which have high parameter spaces (see the comment for explanation)
      • The key point is that P(B) is not just a simple probability of B, but an integral that accounts for all possible parameter values of the model. This integration process is where the effects of model complexity come into play.
      • For simpler models, this integral might be over a smaller space with more concentrated probability mass. For more complex models, the integration occurs over a larger space, often leading to more diffuse probability distributions.
    • The technically correct way to calculate P(B) is to integrate all the posteriors over the entire parameter space = computationally expensive, esp with complex models
      • PyRate uses thermodynamic integration or path sampling to find marginal likelihood, to probabilistically test for the best fitting birth-death model
      • In thermodynamic integration, many MCMC samples are taken from the posterior distributions of the parameters of that model, rather than integrating/sampling from the entire parameter space. There is other stuff about “temperatures” and creating a “path from the prior to the posterior” but not necessary to know.
      • The key function for this is marginal_likelihood(), which is called at the end of the MCMC run if TDI=1. This function integrates over the likelihood values collected during the MCMC to estimate the marginal likelihood.
        • These temperatures are controlled by parameters set with the -k (number of categories) and -a (shape of beta distribution) arguments.
      • The marginal likelihood is output to a file named "*_marginal_likelihood.txt".
    • Used for comparing:
      • Diff birth-death models w/ diff #’s of rate shifts to determine the right # of rate shifts — Bayes Factors, PyRate uses thermodynamic integration to test this
  • PyRate does NOT use P(B) to compare different preservation models to find the best one. Instead: Silvestro 2019 Maximum likelihood test for preservation models is performed before the main Bayesian analysis. It is a preprocessing step that determines which preservation model to use in the main Bayesian analysis. It affects which likelihood function is used for the preservation process, but it does not alter the priors or the fundamental Bayesian approach of the analysis. Models: Preservation Models + Birth-Death Models → jointly estimating S, E, and preservation rates

  1. Preservation models: HPP, NHPP, TPP, Gamma → preservation rates
    1. Poisson processes, used to describe the occurrence of fossil preservation events over time. Poisson processes are used to count occurrences of a particular variable. It assumes the occurrences happen independently of each other, wiaitng times between events are independent (memoryless) of each other, but at an overall average constant rate w/in each time period. That’s good for counting the number of individuals fossilizing at any given time — i.e, the preservation rate
    2. The Poisson processes’ rate parameter is the preservation rate (q) determines the intensity of the Poisson process, and the number of preservation events in a given time interval follows a Poisson distribution with a rate parameter proportional to the preservation rate and the length of the interval.
    3. Models are used to describe the process of fossil preservation + account for biases and incompleteness in the fossil record = models the sampling of the fossil record. This is a major strength of PyRate. By explicitly modelling preservation and sampling processes, PyRate can incorporate incomplete sampling: gaps in the fossil record and the fact that not all species that ever existed are preserved as fossils. It specifically does that by estimating a heterogenous sampling rate q, heterogenous aacross lineages (Gamma) and through time
    4. Models the waiting times between fossil occurrences and the ehterogeneity of preservation rates over time and/or among lineages (depending on the type of model)
    5. Bayes relevance:
      1. P(A) Prior distributions are specified for the preservation rate parameters, representing the prior knowledge or beliefs about their values before considering the data.
      2. The P(B|A) likelihood of the observed fossil occurrences is computed based on the preservation model and the estimated parameters.
      3. The P(A|B) posterior distribution of the preservation rate parameters is obtained by combining the prior distributions and the likelihood using Bayes' theorem.
  2. Birth-Death models: RJMCMC, BDCS (primary 2 models), ADE, MBD, MCDD, Covar, BDC (specialized models for testing specific hypotheses), BDMCMC (not as good as RJMCMC) → S & E rates
    1. Models the diversification process (S + E events) by using continuous-time Markov processes to estimate S and E rates. The states in the Markov chain represent the number of lineages in the system at that time, and transitions occur when lineages are born (speciation) or die (extinction) b/c the number of lineages changes (a new state)
    2. transitions between states (current number of species/lineages, then changes in their number due to transitions from birth → death and death → birth of lineages) continuously over time, with the rates of transition (S and E), determining the probability of moving from one state to another
    3. Markov processes calculate the probability of a change in ‘state’, in this case diversification states `(birth→ death or death → birth).
      1. Bayes relevance:
        1. P(A) Prior distributions are assigned to the speciation and extinction rate parameters, reflecting the prior knowledge or assumptions about their values.
        2. The P(B|A) likelihood of the observed fossil occurrences is calculated based on the birth-death model and the estimated parameters.
        3. The P(A|B) posterior distribution of the speciation and extinction rate parameters is obtained by combining the prior distributions and the likelihood using Bayes' theorem.
    4. It does not use Poisson to inform transitions from birth → death or death → birth because:
      1. Not independent/memoryless: the rates of s and e are based on past and current s and e events. S events increase lineages, which increases s and e, VV for extinction. Waiting times depend on current # of lineages
      2. Non-stationarity: overall rate of events isn’t constant, it changes w/ change in # of lineages.
    5. Birth-Death Model: standard, default
      1. Similar to HPP
      2. Constant S and E rates
    6. Birth-Death Shift Model (BDS): discrete shifts in S and E rates at specific time points
      1. Similar to TPP
      2. # and timing of those rate shifts can be estimated using RJMCMC: Reversible Jump Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm
    7. Time-Dependent Birth-Death Model (BDMCMC): S and E rates vary continuously over time
      1. Adds in diversity-dependence: S and E rates are influenced by the # of extant lineages at each time poiint
    8. BDC: Birth-Death Chronospecies: reconciling differences between phylogenic estimations of diversification rates and fossil data’s estimates of diversification rates by trying different modes of speciation (budding, bifurcation, anagenesis)
      1. 4 params per mode of speciation: s and e for phylogenics and fossils each
      2. Fossil rates should be > = phylogenic rates, and net diversification rates should be equal
    9. Birth-Death Skyline Contemporary (p-sampling):
      1. Focuses only on extant data
      2. Assumes incomplete sampling of extant taxon: Only n extant individuals have been sampled, at a sampling extant taxa probability of p
    10. Multivariate Birth Death Model:
      1. Extends traditional BD models by including multiple predictor variables
      2. Can use BDNN
      3. Output: posterior distributions of s, e, and q with CI’s
        1. Model comparison metrics (marginal likelihoods, Bayes factors)
        2. Estimates of the influence of predictor variables on these rates

    1. The preservation process is modelled as Poisson + the BD is modelled as
    2. continuous-time Markov processes, but BOTH use MCMC methods to estimate their parameters
      1. Prservation Model: q and possibly timing of shits in q’s

Poisson

  1. Independence: Events occur independently of one another.
  2. Stationary: The rate of events is constant over time.
  3. No simultaneity: The probability of two events occurring simultaneously is negligible.
  4. Memoryless: The waiting time until the next event is independent of the time since the last event.

Markov/MCMC

  • Continuous-time Markov Chain: the changes to the system can happen at any time. So the # of cars that have visited a restaurant during the day = the car can arrive at any time. This means car arrivals are independent of each other (Poisson process). This is what our BD models use
    • Vs. Discrete-time Markov Chains: changes to the system have to happen at discrete time intervals, t. Like a board game where the past moves don’t matter, only where the pieces are currently (memory-less, what makes it Markov), and changes to the system can only happen on someone’s turn
  • MCMC: Markov Chain Monte Carlo: great for high dimensional problems, b/c even though it’s a huge parameter space (it contains all possible parameters and therefore all possible models) to explore, the solutions/combos with high posterior probability are very few, so MCMC will start to sample only from those locations of high P(A|B)
    • MCMC is a Bayesian inference technique!!
    • Markov Chain: “random walk” exploration over space, stochastic, step by step, memoryless, dependent only on the current state = very computationally efficient
    • Monte Carlo: repeatedly random sampling from current position to next, to explore the probability distribution, tracking the results to find underlying patterns in data
      • Start with an initial value of P(A), point estimates of all the model parameters = the ‘current state’
      • Proposal step: From current state, ‘hop’ to the next state
        • If you do not have an informative prior to guide the chain, randomly generate a number and hop to the next state
        • OR propose the next step based on a candidate value generated from the proposal distribution (like a prior or posterior that we calculate to guide the mcmc to work fater)
        • Proposal width: if your proposal steps (distance from current state to the next) are too wide, most proposals will be rejected, which means the acceptance rate gets too low, and the chain gets stuck in states for a long period of time.
        • The opposite is true of very narrow proposal steps (which → a lot of autocorrelation, chain won’t explore a wide area). We can possible get over this by setting a ‘lag’ parameter, which
      • Calculate the ‘acceptance probability’, which is a ratio of the posterior probabilities of the previous and current states
        • The acceptance probability is a rule that the user can change
        • If the new state has a higher posterior probability, accept it. If it doesn’t, reject it, don’t move to that next step
      • Repeat in mamy random trials = each random sample is independent b/c don’t have to store info about previous samples. Only needs a new random number, and move on
      • Your sampler’s path from step to step becomes your Markov chain
      • The number of proposed steps (not the actual number of accepted steps) = the number of iterations
      • After you do a ton of iterations, forming a ton of different chains, your paths will converge onto a target distribution (the desired probability distribution), and all your samples collected on the way can be used to estimate properties of the distribution: mean, variance, quantles
        • One single MCMC sample doesn’t tell us anything about uncertainty or variability in parameters, but ALL the samples together do
      • This means the computer can parallelize the process, running multiple random trials (i.e. chains) simultaneously on different processors or cores = divide and conquer
    • What is the parameter space? It includes:
      • Parameters:
        • Preservation rates q
        • S and e rates
        • Times of rate shifts
        • Times of s and e for each lineage
      • Priors: each ^parameter’s probability distribution pre-data. Can be set by user. Defaults are:
        • Preservation rates: Gamma
        • S and e rates: Gamma or Cauchy
        • Times of rate shifts: uniformly distributed within the time range
        • S and e times: uniform within contraints
      • Likelihoods:
        • Preservation likelihood: probability of observed fossil occurrences given that state’s q parameters
        • BD likelihood: probability of that pattern of s and e, given that state’s s and e rates
      • Posterior distributions calculated by the priors and likelihood
    • MCMC Sampler = the computational tool that proposes a new location to move to, based on your current locaiton. It is guided by a proposal distribution
    • Mixing: how well the MCMC sampler explores the posterior distribution of the model’s parameters. Good mixing = the MCMC sampler efficiently traversed the parameter space and converged on the target posterior distribution.
      • Efficient = fast, moving freely throughout the parameter space’s diff regions w/out getting stuck in local nodes
      • Autocorrelation: bad sampling → consecutive samples being highly correlated instead of random → bad exploration of the posterior distriubiton
      • The way you tune the sampler’s moves = tuning its mixing = determines the time it takes to adequately sample the posterior distribution

        • This preserves the shape of the distribution and sets variance =1
        • Sensitive to outliers since it uses mean and std, but not AS sensitive as normalization (min0max scaling)

        • The median is more robust to outliers than the mean! So, subtracting by the median instead of the mean will preserve the original ordinal relationship (spread) of the ordered categories

Workflow:

  • FIRST, the Preservation Model is run: the one chosen by you (HPP, HNPP, TPP, Gamma) takes in the fossil data (no traits) and outputs q, ts, and te estimates
    • HPP = one q rate is sampled, NHPP = one q rate is sampled, TPP = one q rate for each time bin is sampled
      • -mG → those q rates are sampled per lineage
      • -A 4 and no -fixShift → times of rate shifts may be sampled
  • THEN: BDNN replaces the traditional parametric Birth-Death PyRate models (constant-rate, time-variable, DES, CoVar, etc.)
    • It takes fossil data AND traits
  • Ok: for ONE run of the neural network:
    • Inputted fossil data + some predictors
    • And outputted speciation and extinction point estimates
  • ONE MCMC sample: a point estimate of neural network PARAMETERS ONLY (weights, biases, # depending on nn shape)
    • This = one possible configuration of the neural network
    • These parameters do have priors associated with them. If you use hyperparameters like regularization, those also have priors
  • Then: run data through the neural network with that specific set of parameters
    • Get one speciation and extinction point estimate for EACH ROW of the input data (each species in each time bin)
  • Save that parameter info + the outputted estimates
  • Take ANOTHER MCMC sample → run that neural network w/ those params → get another set of outputted target variables
  • At the end:
    • The collection of target variable estimates = one distribution (not a posterior prob dist) of predictions per target variable! You can see how spread out the distribution is, what its mean and variance is, the distribution over just one predictor, etc. I.e, you have an estimate of the range of the target variable and you can group by specific predictors. Buy using MCMC samples of posterior dist parameters, you incorporated uncertainty. In contrast, a standard neural network would only output one point estimate of the target variable
    • MCMC posterior distribution: updated beliefs about the neural network parameters after considering the observed data
  • Following one predictor variable:
    • It has a bunch of data points that are fixed (never touched or changed)
    • All that data is fed through each neural network configuration that the MCMC provides (fed through once for each MCMC sample)
    • The neural network outputs a target variable prediction for each row
    • Each predictor variable also has a gradient — each predictor value has been fixed because they are raw data points. However, all those data points for that predictor is fed through multiple neural network configurations. So, at the end, you have a range of estimated target value for EACH DATA POINT of EACH PREDICTOR.
  • Bayes formula:
    • The prior: Initial beliefs about parameter distributions.
    • The likelihood: How well the network with given parameters explains the data.
    • The posterior: Updated beliefs about parameters after seeing the data.
    • MCMC samples this posterior, making the approach Bayesian.
  • The MCMC is what makes the network “Bayesian”! Instead of providing point estimates of nn parameters like standard nn’s, it provides a range of possible parameters.
  • Each target variables’ distribution is not a posterior prob distribution — it uses the uncertainty of the MCMC to create prediction distribution. Width of it = uncertainty of your predictions. Central tendency
  • Neural Network Output:
    • You're correct that your neural network likely outputs point estimates for the 2 target variables. This is the typical setup for many neural networks, especially in regression tasks.
  • Probability Distribution:
    • In a standard (non-Bayesian) neural network, the network itself doesn't define a probability distribution. However, in the Bayesian context we're discussing, the uncertainty comes from the distribution over the network parameters, not from the network's output directly.
  • What the MCMC is doing:
    • MCMC is sampling different possible configurations of network parameters (weights and biases). Each sample represents one possible set of parameters for your network.
  • How this relates to probability:
    • The collection of MCMC samples represents a distribution over possible network parameters. This indirectly gives us a distribution over possible outputs for any given input.
  • Getting answers about target variables:
    • To get probabilistic information about your target variables, you would:
      • a) Take many MCMC samples (different sets of network parameters)
      • b) For each sample, run your input data through the network
      • c) Collect the outputs for your 2 target variables
      • d) Analyze the distribution of these outputs
        • What users should look at:
          • Central tendency: Mean or median of the outputs across MCMC samples
          • Uncertainty: Credible intervals (e.g., 95% CI) of the outputs
          • Full distribution: Histogram or kernel density estimate of the outputs
  • Interpreting results:
    • The spread of outputs across MCMC samples represents the model's uncertainty
    • A narrow distribution suggests high confidence in the prediction
    • A wide distribution indicates more uncertainty
  • Example:

      • The mean might be 22.5°C
      • The 95% credible interval might be [21°C, 24°C]
      • This gives you both a point estimate (22.5°C) and a measure of uncertainty

# BDNN Neural Network Architecture and Components

## Key Insight

First, it's important to understand that ts, te, and q are not inside the neural networks - they are external parameters that interact with the network's outputs. BDNN actually consists of two separate neural networks:

1. A speciation network that outputs species-specific speciation rates (λ)

2. An extinction network that outputs species-specific extinction rates (μ)

## Network Inputs

Each neural network takes as input:

1. Species traits (e.g., body size, diet, etc.)

2. Environmental predictors (e.g., temperature, CO2)

3. Time (as a continuous variable)

The input layer consists of nodes representing each of these features:

```

X = [x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ]

where:

- x₁ to xₖ are trait values

- x_{k+1} to x_{m} are environmental values

- x_{m+1} is time

```

## Hidden Layers

Each hidden layer performs the following computation:

```

h₁ = σ(W₁X + b₁)

h₂ = σ(W₂h₁ + b₂)

...

hₗ = σ(Wₗh_{ℓ-1} + bₗ)

where:

- σ is the activation function (often ReLU)

- Wᵢ are the weight matrices

- bᵢ are the bias vectors

- hᵢ are the hidden layer outputs

```

## Output Layer

Each network has a single output node that produces a rate:

- Speciation network: λ (lambda) = species-specific speciation rate

- Extinction network: μ (mu) = species-specific extinction rate

The final computation is:

```

rate = σ_output(W_final * h_final + b_final)

where σ_output ensures the rate is positive (often exponential or softplus)

```

## External Components

### Times (ts, te) and Sampling Rates (q)

These are not network parameters but rather:

- ts, te: Parameters updated during MCMC

- q: Sampling rates from preservation model

### Likelihood Calculations

The preservation likelihood is calculated outside the network:

```

PP_lik = P(fossil occurrences | ts, te, q)

```

The birth-death likelihood uses the network outputs:

```

BD_lik = P(ts, te | λ, μ)

where:

- λ comes from speciation network

- μ comes from extinction network

```

## Data Flow and Processing

1. For each species at each time point:

a. Gather input features

b. Pass through both networks

c. Get species-specific λ and μ

2. During MCMC:

a. Update ts/te/q OR network weights

b. If updating ts/te/q:

- Recalculate relevant likelihoods

- Use cached network outputs

c. If updating weights:

- Run affected network

- Get new rates

- Calculate BD_lik

## Species-Specific Rates

The rates are the direct outputs of the networks. For any species i at time t:

```

λᵢ(t) = speciation_network(traits_i, env(t), t)

μᵢ(t) = extinction_network(traits_i, env(t), t)

```

These rates then feed into the birth-death likelihood calculation.

## Full Forward Pass Example

For a single species at one time point:

```

# Input preparation

X = [

trait_1, trait_2, ..., # Species traits

temp(t), co2(t), ..., # Environmental values at time t

t # Time point

]

# Network computations (speciation network example)

h1 = ReLU(W1 @ X + b1) # First hidden layer

h2 = ReLU(W2 @ h1 + b2) # Second hidden layer

...

λ = exp(W_final @ h_final + b_final) # Output layer (speciation rate)

# Similar process for extinction network to get μ

```

## Important Notes

1. Network Independence

- Speciation and extinction networks are separate

- They share input structure but have independent weights

- Only one network is updated per MCMC iteration

2. Rate Generation

- Networks generate instantaneous rates

- Rates vary continuously with time and traits

- Rates are always positive due to output activation

3. Likelihood Integration

- Network outputs (λ, μ) feed into BD_lik

- ts, te, q feed into PP_lik

- Both likelihoods contribute to MCMC acceptance

4. Computational Efficiency

- Layer outputs are cached when possible

- Only affected portions are recalculated

- Likelihoods are updated selectively

This architecture allows BDNN to learn complex relationships between traits, environment, and diversification rates while maintaining the constraints of birth-death processes and accounting for preservation bias.

Some background:

During the MCMC, a single generation is an update to either the times of speciation and extinction (logged as TS and TE), the sampling rates q, or the weights of one of the layers of the neural net (never all of these together because such big changes will not be accepted in the mcmc).

We have two neural nets, one for speciation and one for extinction. When their weights are updated, we need to go from their input (the species traits, time, and the environmental predictors) through all layers to get the species specific rates (we use them to get the birth-death likelihood). Going through the network involves matrix by matrix multiplications for each layer (i.e. dot products or tensor multiplication) and they are computationally demanding.

Previously, we updated at the same MCMC generation the neural nets for speciation and extinction. Now, we only update one of them and thus safe half of the computation cost. Because we only go through on neural net, there is also nothing to parallelize as before this was concurrently done for the two neural nets of speciation and extinction.

The second trick to make the BDNN faster is that if we update the weights of e.g. the 2nd layer of the neural net we do the matrix by matrix operations only form the 2nd layer onward (as the input to the 2nd layer is not changed and had been computed previously).

NN Analysis

  • At the output layer, we compare the output vector to the ground truth. We calculate a cost/loss function: a measure of error like the squared differences between the output and the ground truth (ex: MSE or rMSE)
  • Then we backpropogate through the NN, updating the network weights by reducing or increasing each of them by a small increment, in order to optimize the loss function (lower it)
  • We create a high-dimensional space called the “gradient” that contains all those weights, and the loss of the network at each point in that space (at each possible combination of weights). And we take tiny steps in whichever direction (changing weights by a small amount), following whichever direction led to a lower loss function
    • In reality, we don’t just follow the step that led to a decrease in the loss function. We follow the step that had the steepest slope — the fastest decrease, then continue until the slope is 0 (loss cannot decrease anymore, we think)
  • SHAP value: per-feature, the direction and magnitude of the feature’s affect on each individual prediction. Obviously can be aggregated into overall value for all predictions. Can be plotted in various ways, which is super helpful. Use “shap” library
  • Feature importance: how important each feature was to the model itself
  • Permutation: rearranging the order of one feature (predictor). So we have a bunch of fossil occurrences, and columns (features) for age, size, habitat, etc. The target is speciation rate.
    • We would first get predictions with all the data unchanged
    • We would “permute” the age feature by rearraching/randomly shuffling the values in that column → breaking its relationship with the target
    • We would run the BDNN again. If the predictions are much more wrong (MSE is higher), then the “age” feature is very important.
      • Importance score = MSE of model when “age” is permuted - MSE of model w/out permutations
    • We would run permutations of “age” multiple times to get a stable estimate, and average the resulting importance score
    • We do multiple permutations of every feature column, then compare their importance scores. The highest one = the most important

Deep Dive:

  • Linear regression is a NN, where coeff is a network weight, x is predictor, intercept b is average across species
  • Issues with linear regressions: linear only unless exponents added, no interactions among predictors unless defined.
  • Benefit with linear regressions: has interpretable effect sizes, no black box, test statistics available
  • Neural network:
    • Unlike linear where each input has one weight (coefficient), each input variable has a weight per node in the next hidden layer
    • Each input goes to each node in the hidden layer = allowing interactions between all of the inputs
    • Activation function in the hidden layer = a simple, specifically non-linear (literally any shape, you don’t need to specify) function
  • Cons:
    • Outputs aren’t directly interpretable like there are in regression models
    • Over-parameterized/overfit
  • Fossil record is our only direct evidence of past biodiversity, however
  • Fossil record has a ton of non-random biases at taxonomic, temporal, spatial scales that makes true patterns of biodiversity unidentifiable by current methods (close et al 2020)
    • Current methods (rarefaction) assume random sampling, which is obv wrong
    • Close et. al marine diversity — 30-40% something
    • New study suggests sampling heterogeneity accounts for 50-60% of changes in even sampling-standardized global biodiversity/richness estimates
    • We need to focus explicitly on studies of diversity through space and time (background papers in ‘Academic Papers’ notes doc!)
    • Variable preservation rates across lineages + their effect on unobserved lineages also a problem
  • Deep dive: fossils → biodiversity over time
  • DeepDive numerical inputs: all of which can be grouped by space/area and time bin. Model knows the time bins are interrelated as a time-series
    • Sampled diversity
    • Range-through diversity
    • # of occurrences
    • # of singletons
    • # of sampling localities
  • Output → global diversity estimate for a clade for each time bin
  • Obviously if we used real data to train, it would result in an unsupervised model b/c we don’t have ground truths (real diversity estimates that we know for sure)
  • So: simulated data where we know the real diversity patterns, then we simulate an artificial fossil record w/ any type/number of biases we want to add in (spatial, temporal, but mainly regional heterogeneities, w/ fossil occurrences distributed across discrete regions and through time) which is “degrading the data”
    • So we can train the NN on the simulated data, compare its predicted diversity to true biodiversity
    • 10’s-100’s of thousands of simulated datasets

  • Then we feed empirical datasets (not simulated) and feed into the trained model
  • Crucial that the simulations are calibrated to the real data, that it resembles that dataset, is within it’s range/in the right ballpark, so that the trained model can extrapolate properly, resulting in reliable estimates
    • Interpolation (when simulated data does match raw) vs. extrapolation (when simulated data doesn’t match raw)
    • Do this by comparing the features of the empirical dataset with the features of the simulated datasets: frequencies of taxa, occurrences, singletons, localities, etc.
  • We can even add in biases that aren’t necessarily observable in the fossil record, from a priori external knowledge like changes in land mass locations, inferred mass extinction events, current # of extant species, diversity dependence
    • Ex: they required a simulation for a splicific clade to start at a specific timefrmae, in only 5 continental regions, w/ an end constraint of at least 3 extant species
  • In the study:
    • Benefit: You can run other methods like SQS on the simulated data, then compare its rMSE to DeepDive’s
    • Compared to SQS:
      • SQS estimates relative diversity, so using rMSE and R2 to compare the two
      • Outperformed, lower relative median rMSE, higher median coff of determination R2 (0.42 vs 0.96)
      • Better w/ gaps in data, low preservation, low completeness, sudden and smooth diversity changes, completeness (coverage)
      • Improvement in all bias situations, but biggest in spatial bias
    • More error when lifespan of clades was shorter
    • No effect:
      • Extinct vs. extant clades

      • Extant data can be used, which means that we can calibrate the NN to return predictions that exactly reflect the true diversity we see today in those living species

        • Simulations have features like: taxa, occurrences, singletons, localities, with features that are calibrated to be close to that of the empirical dataset (so the model is being trained on simulations w/ biases akin to the empirical set)
      • Deep Learning Module (Model Training): Train the Recurrent Neural Network model on the features from those simulations, run on a GPU to make it fast
        • RNN: LSTM’s = estimate for a time bin depends not only on that bin’s data, but also on successor and predecessor’s data

  • Can we use these predictions of diversity to uncover the CAUSES/DRIVERS of those diversity changes?
  • We solve this by using a PyRate Birth-Death model that estimates the strength of the predictors that shape those curves
    • BD s and e rates → diversification through time
    • Types:
      • Constant rate vs. time-variable models
      • Rates can be a function of a discrete trait: ADE or Trait-Dependent extinction
      • Multivariate BD model: multiple linear regression model
      • Diversity-dependence w/in and between clades
    • Problem:
      • We need to specify the variables you want to correlate with s and rates
      • And you need to specify the shape of the relationship of that variable w/ s and e
      • And you can’t let variables interact w/ each other
    • Solution: BD model → Birth-Death Neural Network
      • Any features inputted → species and time specific s and e rates
      • All the benefits of all NN
      • Features can be time, time-series data, categorical or continuous traits, etc.
      • Fits exactly into existing PyRate, replacing existing BD model
    • Overfitting/overparameterization issue → spurious outputs
      • This has lower risk than DeepDive b/c we aren’t simulating data, we’re taking empirical inputs and analyzing it w/ Bayes
      • But other solutions: regularization
        • Put priors (a priori assumptions) on Neural Network weights: normal distributions on the coefficients. We favor a null model where the coeffs have no effect, since the normal distribution is centered on 0
        • Bayesian shrinkage, shrinking the parameters around the mean: raise all rates to one parameter, default set to 1 = parameters unchanged
          • Closer to 0 = more flattened/closer to 0/closer to no effects
      • → Outputs species-specific s and e rates
        • eXplainable AI is a program that ranks the predictors by most predictive/most important in driving changes in s and e rates
          • In study’s graphs:
          • Found s rate was most impacted by time and trait
          • Found e rate was most impacted by overlapping with humans
        • Measures used to do this:
          • Permutations (Partial Dependence Plots): plots the magnitude of effects of these predictors
          • Marginal probabilities
          • SHAP values: mean absolute deviation from the mean rate across all species caused by a predictor
